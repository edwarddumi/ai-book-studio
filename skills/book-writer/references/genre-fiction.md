# Fiction Book Guide

## Core Principle
Every scene must do at least two things simultaneously: advance the plot AND reveal character (or raise stakes, or deepen theme). If a scene only does one thing, cut or rewrite it.

## Story Structure Options

### Three-Act Structure
- **Act 1** (≈25%): Establish world, protagonist, and status quo → Inciting incident → First turning point
- **Act 2** (≈50%): Rising conflict, midpoint reversal, darkest moment → Second turning point
- **Act 3** (≈25%): Final confrontation → Climax → Resolution

### Five-Act (for epic/literary fiction)
Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Denouement

### Hero's Journey (for adventure, fantasy, coming-of-age)
Ordinary World → Call to Adventure → Refusal → Mentor → Crossing Threshold → Tests → Ordeal → Reward → Road Back → Resurrection → Return

## Chapter Structure
- **Opening line**: the best sentence in the chapter — make it earn its place
- **Scene goal**: every scene has a character who wants something
- **Conflict**: something must resist, complicate, or prevent
- **Ending**: either a win that creates a new problem, or a loss that raises stakes

## POV Rules
- **First person**: intimate, limited — we only know what the narrator knows
- **Third limited**: close to one character per scene; shift carefully
- **Third omniscient**: flexible but risky — can feel distant; use sparingly
- **Second person**: rare, powerful for immersion; commits the reader

## Dialogue Rules
- Every line of dialogue must do work: reveal character, advance plot, or create tension
- People don't speak in complete sentences — use fragments, interruptions, deflections
- Subtext: characters rarely say what they mean; show what's underneath
- Attribution: "said" is invisible; use action beats instead of adverbs ("He crossed his arms" not "he said angrily")

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Info-dumping in the first chapter
- Describing characters through mirrors
- Starting with weather or waking up
- Passive protagonist — they must make choices that drive the story
- Telling emotions instead of showing behavior ("She was angry" → "She slammed the cabinet.")

## Chapter Endings
- End on a hook, a question, a revelation, or an emotional moment
- The reader must feel compelled to start the next chapter
- Avoid tidy, resolved chapter endings — leave something unresolved
