# Self-Help & Personal Development Book Guide

## The Core Promise
Every self-help book makes an implicit contract with the reader: "If you read and apply this, your life will be better in this specific way." Name that promise explicitly in Chapter 1 and deliver it by the final chapter.

## Structure Patterns

### The Transformation Arc
Reader starts broken/stuck → learns the framework → applies it → becomes transformed. Each chapter is a step in that transformation.

### The Pillar Model
3-7 core pillars, each gets 2-3 chapters. Works well for habit books, productivity systems, mindset work.

### Problem → Solution → Integration
Open each chapter with the problem the reader recognizes in themselves. Offer the solution. Show integration into real life.

## Chapter Structure (recommended)
1. **Relatable opening** — a scenario the reader sees themselves in
2. **Name the pain** — what this problem costs them
3. **The insight** — the reframe or principle
4. **The practice** — concrete steps to apply it
5. **Common resistance** — address the "but what about…" the reader is already thinking
6. **Reflection prompt or exercise** — makes the book interactive

## Voice Guidelines
- Warm but direct — like advice from a smart, caring friend
- Use "you" constantly — make it feel personal
- First person from the author is fine; don't hide behind "one" or "people"
- Share your own failures, not just your wins — vulnerability = trust

## Must-Include Elements
- Exercises, journaling prompts, or reflection questions
- Success stories (real or composite, labeled honestly)
- A clear framework the reader can remember and reference
- Permission to be imperfect — self-help readers are often hard on themselves

# How-To & Instructional Book Guide

## Core Principle
The reader has a specific problem or goal. Your job is to take them from where they are to where they want to be — clearly, efficiently, and without wasted words.

## Structure
- Linear: each chapter builds on the previous — Chapter 4 assumes they've done Chapters 1-3
- Modular: each chapter is standalone — readers can jump to what they need

## Chapter Structure
1. **What we're doing and why** (brief — 100-200 words max)
2. **What you need** — materials, knowledge, tools, time
3. **Step-by-step instructions** — numbered, specific, unambiguous
4. **Troubleshooting** — what goes wrong and how to fix it
5. **What's next** — bridge to the next chapter or skill

## Voice Guidelines
- Active voice, imperative tense: "Open the file" not "The file should be opened"
- Short sentences. One instruction per sentence.
- Use numbered lists for sequences; bullet points for options or alternatives
- Screenshots, diagrams, or described visuals every 500-800 words

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming knowledge the reader doesn't have — define every term on first use
- Skipping steps that feel obvious to you but aren't to the reader
- Burying the most important warning in the middle of a paragraph — use call-out boxes or bold
- Not testing the instructions — walk through them yourself as a beginner would
